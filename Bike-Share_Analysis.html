Executive Summary

This analysis was prepared for Lily Moreno, Director of Marketing at Cyclistic. Cyclistic's finance team already confirmed it: annual members generate more profit per rider than casual users. The strategic question is not whether to convert casual riders -- it is which casual riders to target, when, and with what message.

This analysis of 791,264 Q1 rides across 2019 and 2020 shows that casual riders and annual members use the system in fundamentally different ways. Members are commuters. Casual riders are weekend leisure cyclists. Those two groups need different conversion arguments, different timing, and different product structures to move them toward membership.

The highest-value conversion opportunity is not a discount. It is a product that matches how casual riders actually use the system -- a leisure or weekend membership tier that reduces the commitment barrier and creates a natural upsell path to annual membership.

Analysis

Four behavioral differences worth examining for conversion strategy

01Members commute. Casual riders explore. These require different conversion arguments.

Members account for 91% of Q1 rides (720,126) while casual riders represent 9% (71,138). But casual riders take rides that average 36.5 minutes versus 11.4 minutes for members -- a 3.2x gap. Members show the classic dual-peak commute pattern: 7-9 AM and 5-6 PM on weekdays. Casual riders show a single broad midday peak on weekends.

Interpretation for marketing and product

A casual rider taking a 36-minute Saturday ride does not need a commuter pitch. Messaging focused on cost savings per trip, city exploration, and no per-minute charges on longer rides is more relevant than "save time on your commute." The conversion argument needs to match the actual use case -- not the member use case the brand already serves well.

02The conversion window is Saturday and Sunday, 10 AM to 4 PM

Casual ridership peaks Saturday and Sunday. Member ridership drops sharply on those same days. The overlap is minimal -- these are two distinct populations using the same infrastructure at different times.

What this suggests for campaign timing

The optimal conversion window is mid-ride on a Saturday or Sunday -- when a casual rider is actively engaged with the product and physically present at a docking station. In-app prompts, docking station signage, and push notifications triggered by weekend ride completion have higher conversion probability than weekday email campaigns. Targeting the moment of highest product engagement, not the moment of highest digital attention, is the correct timing framework.

03Casual riders who use the system frequently likely pay more annually than members do

At casual single-ride rates of approximately $3.30 per trip (Divvy 2019-2020 pricing), a rider taking two rides per weekend across 12 months spends roughly $343 annually. Annual membership at $108/year (Divvy 2019 rate) saves that rider approximately $235. The math favors membership for any casual rider using the system more than 33 times per year -- less than once per week. Note: these are approximate 2019 Divvy pricing figures; current rates should be substituted before using this estimate in any live campaign.

What this suggests for conversion messaging

A cost comparison calculator -- showing what a casual rider's Q1 ride pattern would have cost at casual rates vs. what annual membership would have cost -- is the most direct conversion argument available. It is specific to the rider's actual behavior, not a hypothetical. This requires knowing individual ride history per user, which the current dataset's privacy constraints prevent. A cohort-level version (e.g., "riders who took 8+ rides in Q1 paid an estimated $X vs. $Y for annual membership") is achievable with the current data and could be surfaced in-app.

04Casual ridership is more seasonal -- which means conversion timing matters by month

Casual ridership grows more steeply as Q1 progresses into warmer months. Members maintain more consistent ridership through the same period. Casual riders are weather-sensitive in a way members are not -- consistent with leisure use patterns and a likely tourist component.

What this suggests for campaign calendar

Conversion campaigns launched in late Q1 (March) reach casual riders at the moment their ridership is increasing and the weather is improving. Waiting for Q2 peak summer ridership means competing with tourism marketing noise and higher acquisition costs. The optimal conversion window is early in the season -- before casual riders have settled into their summer pattern without membership.

Action Items

Three conversion levers ranked by expected reach

Highest Reach

Weekend conversion campaigns at peak usage moments

Target casual riders at docking stations and via in-app prompt on Saturday and Sunday. Trigger the conversion offer immediately after ride completion -- the highest-engagement moment. A time-limited discount for converting during or after a weekend ride reduces friction at the point of maximum product satisfaction.

Timing: ride completion trigger, Saturday and Sunday, 10 AM to 4 PM

Product

Create a leisure or weekend membership tier

Annual membership is priced and positioned for commuters. A "Weekend Pass" or "Leisure Membership" priced between casual rates and annual membership reduces the commitment barrier. Casual riders in this tier create an upsell path to annual membership and generate revenue that currently does not exist.

Pricing target: between casual per-trip rates and annual membership cost

Digital

Run leisure-focused ads between 10 AM and 4 PM on weekends

Messaging should focus on exploration and longer-ride value, not commuting efficiency. Partner with local tourism and event platforms to reach casual riders planning weekend activities. Match the channel timing to when casual riders are active -- not when members are.

Creative angle: "Explore more, pay less" not "Save time on your commute"

Scope Limitations

This analysis covers Q1 only (January to March). Full-year data would strengthen seasonal findings, particularly summer leisure riding peaks. Data privacy prevents linking rides to individual users -- lifetime value comparisons and funnel tracking are not possible with this dataset. A/B testing the weekend conversion campaign against a control group is recommended before full rollout.
